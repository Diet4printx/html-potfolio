In conclusion; Students today believe in their technology skills as a result of being familiar with technology in their daily lives. However, there is a need to examine students’ perception about their technology skills to determine what the students’ technology skill levels actually are; especially their prior knowledge in relation to school-related expectations. Indeed, the technology skills that students need to apply in their learning are not necessarily related to those they use in their social lives and may not transfer properly when used for knowledge building. Moreover, the real meaning of technology literacy is not about the ability to play a game, do basic word processing, send e-mail or browse the Web. These skills are not sufficient for students to be considered technology literate. It is about the meaningful use of these skills to enhance their learning. On the other hand, students can integrate technology skills in their learning when they use technology as tools for organization, communication, research, and problem solving. Also, these skills cannot be practiced separately but should be a part of each instructional event during school, in any subject (Eisenberg, Johnson, & Berkowitz, 2010). Using technology as a tool is a fundamental concept to improve students learning. Thus, teachers should accentuate optimal tactics for using educational technology, through their instruction, and provide the opportunities that will result in students gaining improved technology skills.
